# What the ADA is

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) is a U.S. civil-rights law that prohibits
discrimination against people with disabilities. The Department of Justice (DOJ) has
long taken the position that the ADA's promise of equal access extends to the goods
and services that state and local governments and businesses offer to the public on
the web.

# Why digital accessibility matters

When a website, mobile app, or embedded tool cannot be used by people who rely on
screen readers, keyboard navigation, captions, or other assistive technology, those
users can be denied access to programs and services that everyone else can reach.
DOJ guidance highlights features that frequently create barriers: poor color
contrast, missing text alternatives, uncaptioned video, unlabeled form controls,
content that cannot be operated by keyboard, and pages that break when zoomed.

# How the ADA relates to Section 508 and WCAG

The ADA is the civil-rights law. Section 508 is a separate federal law about the
technology federal agencies build and buy. The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines
(WCAG), published by the W3C, are the technical standard both regimes point to as the
practical measure of an accessible digital experience.

# Note on legal interpretation

This page summarizes the ADA's reach into digital services at a high level. Specific
applicability to a given organization is a legal determination; counsel should
confirm Title II or Title III exposure for your situation.
